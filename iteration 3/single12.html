    Gilt gold and silver Sachet

    • Dynasty: Tang
    • Category: daily necessities
    • Museum: National Museum of China
    • Unearthed: Xi'an, Shaanxi Province

    The sachet is composed of two hemispheres, with a child and mother mouth that can be buckled. The lower hemisphere is equipped with two concentric rings and a fragrant bowl containing spices. The large machine ring is connected with the outer ball wall, and the small machine ring is connected with the large machine ring and the incense cup respectively. When in use, due to the gravity of the incense bowl itself and the mechanical balance of the two concentric rings, no matter how the sachet rolls, the incense bowl inside can remain horizontal and the spices will not be poured. The shell of the sachet is carved with flower and bird patterns, which is not only beautiful, but also sends out the fragrance through these hollows. There is a chain on the sachet and a hook on the upper part, which is convenient to wear and can also be hung in the indoor tent. There are 4 pieces in total. They are basically the same size and shape. They should be made by one person or workshop. They are dated to the middle of the 8th century.

    The diameter of this incense utensil is mostly about 5cm, but one unearthed from the Taji underground palace of Famen Temple is 12.8cm wide. A piece collected by masakura court in Japan is 18 cm wide and 18.8 cm high. As for its name, it is called the sachet in the material account of Famen Temple. It can be seen that the sachets are not necessarily made of silk. During the an Shi rebellion, Emperor Xuanzong of Tang Dynasty was forced to kill Yang Guifei on the way to escape and bury her hastily. More than a year later, Xuanzong returned to Chang'an and secretly ordered Gao Lishi to dig and bury. According to records, at that time, Yang Guifei "had bad skin, but the sachet was still there". Yang Guifei is probably wearing this kind of metal sachet.
