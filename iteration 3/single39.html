    Carbonized rice

    • Dynasty: Neolithic Age
    • Category: grain
    • Museum: National Museum of China
    • Unearthed: Hemudu site, Zhejiang

    Rice is an artificial variety produced by the cultivation and improvement of wild rice. These carbonized rice were unearthed in the fourth cultural layer of Hemudu site. Because the accumulation horizon is submerged below the water level and isolated from the air, when the rice is unearthed, the grain is still golden yellow, the shape is intact, the awn is clear, and the rice rod is green and straight. The rice grain is nearly oval, which is quite different from wild rice.

    The house where Hemudu people live is a so-called dry fence type building - piles are buried on the ground, wood floors are added on it, and then houses are built on it. Under the floor of the dry fence building, archaeologists found a large number of deposits including rice, rice husk, rice straw, rice leaves and reeds, with a thickness of about 20-50 cm and a maximum thickness of more than 1 meter. If the accumulated rice remains are converted into fresh rice, it can reach tens of thousands of kilograms.

    In addition to the above deposits, archaeologists also found a large number of bones used to level paddy fields and repair ditches at Hemudu site, and an ancient paddy field site was also found at Tianluoshan site near Hemudu. All these evidences show that Hemudu people have taken rice as their staple food, and rice agriculture has entered a relatively mature stage of development. The discovery of such a large-scale rice remains in the middle Neolithic age at Hemudu site fully proves that China is the hometown of rice.
