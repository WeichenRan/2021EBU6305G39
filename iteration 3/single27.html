    Large Dragon Stamps

    • Dynasty: Qing
    • Category: daily necessities
    • Museum: National Museum of China
    • Size: 2.25cm long&wide

    This set of three stamps has a face value of 1 cent silver, 3 cents silver and 5 cents silver respectively. In the middle of the pattern is the image of a giant dragon lined with clouds. English China is listed in the upper frame, and English candarin (s) with a face value of a few cents of silver is listed in the lower frame. The words "big" and "Qing" are listed in the upper two corners.

    In 1878, Hurd, a Briton from the general tax department, appointed de Cuilin, the tax department of Tianjin Customs, to take Tianjin as the center, try to run Daqing post in Beijing, Tianjin, Yantai, Niuzhuang and Shanghai, and follow the Western postal system to establish a modern postal system in China. Subsequently, de Cuilin entrusted Jiang customs (now Shanghai Customs) to print 3-cent silver and 5-cent silver stamps, and printed another 1-cent silver. Thus, the first set of stamps in the history of China Post - "Customs dragon stamps" was born.

    It is still a mystery who designed the customs dragon stamps. For a long time, people thought that the designer of this set of stamps was Morse, a British working in Chinese customs, but this statement was later denied by researchers. The Dragon stamps may have come from an unknown Chinese artist.
