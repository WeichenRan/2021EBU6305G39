    Eastern Roman coins

    • Dynasty: Tang
    • Category: Currency
    • Museum: National Museum of China
    • Unearthed: Xianyang City, Shaanxi Province

    The obverse of the gold coin is the bust of the king, and the inscription starts from the right-hand side of the king, which means "our Lord Justin II, eternal emperor". On the back is a goddess in armor, and the inscription under its seat means that it was printed and cast in Constantinople. Starting from the lower right side of the statue, there are also inscriptions arranged from bottom to top, almost around, which means "victory of the supreme masters". The last e is the mint mark.

    Byzantine gold coins and their imitations are the most important relics of Eastern Rome found in China. There are about 40 pieces, mainly concentrated in Xinjiang, Inner Mongolia, Ningxia, Shaanxi, Hebei, Henan and other places. According to documents, in the Northern Zhou Dynasty, "the Hexi counties used gold and silver money from the western regions", and the "gold and silver money" may include this kind of Eastern Roman gold coin. On the gold coin is the bust of Justin II, which is different from the Byzantine emperor who carried the spear. He held the earth in his right hand and the shield in his left hand. The goddess on the back sat in a sitting position and held the earth in his hand. There is a cross on the earth, which is a symbol of Christian rule of the world. This seated image was created by Justin II and has never been used again.
