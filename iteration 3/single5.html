    Copper shaft head

    • Dynasty: Warring States period
    • Category: bronze ware
    • Museum: National Museum of China
    • Unearthed: Hui county, Henan Province

    This bronze lantern is in the shape of a horse's head. Its head and neck are inlaid with gold ornaments with curly hair and scale patterns. It is exquisitely made. It is one of the typical representative works of inlaid gold, silver and bronze ware in the Warring States period. Wei was a great aristocrat of Jin in the spring and Autumn period, and later gradually became a powerful political force. In 471 BC, Han, Zhao and Wei eliminated other forces and divided the state of Jin. In 403 BC, Wei was canonized as a "vassal" by King Weilie of Zhou, becoming a powerful country in the Central Plains during the Warring States period. Wei's territory was in du'anyi in the southwest of Shanxi Province and the north of Henan Province, and then moved to Daliang. At the beginning of the Warring States period, marquis Wen of Wei appointed Li Xuan, Wu Qi and Ximen Bao to carry out economic, political and military reforms, which accelerated the feudalization process of Wei and made Wei a famous power in the early Warring States period. The tomb of Guwei village in Huixian county is a large-scale "middle" shaped tomb, surrounded by a cemetery, which is similar to the specifications of the tombs of princes, so the unearthed chariot shafts are extremely exquisite.

    This bronze horse head with gold and silver was unearthed in the tomb of the nobles of the state of Wei in the middle of the Warring States period. This tomb is the highest specification among the tombs of the state of Wei. The unearthed artifacts are exquisite and exquisite. This bronze with gold and silver inlaid represents the highest level of bronze casting and gold and silver inlaid technology in the state of Wei, and also reflects the extravagance of noble life.
