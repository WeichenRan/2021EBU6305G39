    Jade seal of Gu

    • Dynasty: Qin
    • Category: Jade
    • Museum: National Museum of China
    • Size: height 1.6cm, side length 2.2cm

    The jade is white and the altar button. The printing surface is square, and the white seal character "Gongsun Valley seal" is written. The four character seal is engraved in the "Tian" shaped square, and the single line of button and printed edge is Yin engraved with cloud pattern. The seal is exquisitely made, with smooth lines, fine jade, white and moist. The hanging cloud pattern on it is basically the same as the hanging cloud pattern on the Qin Dynasty Jade high foot cup unearthed from the Afang Palace site in chezhang village, the western suburb of Xi'an, Shaanxi Province. The printing font is "Qin Zhuan". The Qin seal characters have slightly longer glyphs, longer strokes, thin lines, and many round turns at the turning points.

    Although the Qin Dynasty was only 15 years old, the characters were unified and called Xiaozhuan. Li Si, the Prime Minister of the Qin Dynasty, also stipulated the seal and set up a "Fu Jie Ling Cheng" in charge of the Shaofu government. From the Qin Dynasty, it was stipulated that only the seals of the emperor, Empress and princes could be called "Xi" as a symbol of power. The common people's seal is called "seal" or "seal". The word "Yin" in ancient seals began in the Qin Dynasty. Qin seal is not handed down much, and jade seal is even rarer.

    The official seal of the Qin Dynasty has the same shape and size, unlike the ancient seal. Both official and private seals are chiseled in white. Official seals have boundary bars, general officials have "field" boundary bars, and low-level officials have "day" boundary bars. The seal characters of the Qin Dynasty are all unified in small seal characters, so the structure of the seal is roughly straight and square, unlike the six kingdoms characters scattered in the ancient seal. This seal is an important material material for studying the seal cutting and calligraphy of the Qin Dynasty.
