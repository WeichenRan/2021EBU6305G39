    Zhenghe bell

    • Dynasty: Ming
    • Category: ritual vessels
    • Museum: National Museum of China
    • Unearthed: Caiji, Fujian

    The bell is brownish green, in the shape of a cauldron, with a sunflower mouth; The button of the clock is a double dragon handle. Twelve groups of cloud Qi Ruyi patterns are floating on the surface of the shoulder of the clock. The motif in the middle of the belly is cloud water wave patterns, and inscriptions, eight trigrams, cloud thunder and other characters are also cast; The upper part of the main ornamentation is surrounded by eight trigrams in five groups, of which the second and fourth groups are respectively cast with the inscriptions of "national peace and people's safety" and "good weather". The inscription on the lower part of the bronze bell reads: "on the auspicious day of the midsummer of 1911, when Xuande was six years old in the Ming Dynasty, eunuch Zheng He and Wang Jinghong, together with officials and soldiers, made a heart to cast a bronze bell for eternal longevity, and prayed for peace and good luck in returning to the West."

    In the sixth year of Xuande (1431 AD), Zheng He was ordered to sail to the "West" for the seventh time. He arrived in Changle, Fujian Province in March to wait for the winter monsoon. In May, he went up along the Minjiang River to Nanping Town, cast this bronze bell and Bushi Temple, and prayed for safe navigation to the sea. In November of the same year, he sailed away from Changle. It was originally placed in the Sanbao hall about 5 miles outside Yichao village in maodi, Nanping City, Fujian Province. After the hall was destroyed, it was moved to Huaguang temple in Yichao village. There are two different opinions on the casting location of the bronze bell. One is that it was cast by Zheng He when he built the Sanqing palace in Changle, Fujian Province, and later transported from Changle to Nanping for some reason; Another view is that Zheng He once went to Nanping and cast bronze bells there.
